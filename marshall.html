Divide and Conquer

Let $a$, $b$, $k$, and $n$ be integers with $n>0$ and $k>0$. If $a \equiv b \pmod{n}$, then \[a^k \equiv b^k \pmod{n}\]
Prove by induction. Use Exercise 1.17 for the inductive step.
Does $ac \equiv bc \pmod{n}$ imply $a \equiv b \pmod{n}$?
No. Take $(a,b,c,n) = (1,3,2,4)$. I am too lazy to write out $a = 1, b = 3, c = 2, n = 4$. I might use this notation later but this is not standard.
Let a natural number $n$ be expressed in base 10 as \[n = a_ka_{k-1}\dots a_0\] If $m = \sum_{i=0}^k a_i$, then $n \equiv m \pmod{3}$.
Since $10 \equiv 1 \pmod{3}$, by Theorem 1.18 it follows $10^i \equiv 1 \pmod{3}$ for all positive integers $i$. Moreover, it's trivially true when $i = 0$. Hence, $10^j \equiv 1 \pmod{3}$ for all non-negative integers $j$. It follows $10^ia_i \equiv a_i \pmod{3}$ for all $i = 0,1,\dots,k$. Then by Lemma 1.21.1 we have \[\sum_{i=0}^k 10^ia_i \equiv \sum_{i=0}^k a_i \pmod{3}\] Hence, $n \equiv m \pmod{3}$
Let $m \in \mathbb{N}$ and $n$ be a positive integer. Suppose we have integers $a_1,a_2,\dots,a_m$ and $b_1,b_2,\dots,b_m$ such that $a_i \equiv b_i \pmod{n}$ for all $i$. Then $\sum a_i \equiv \sum b_i \pmod{n}$.
Sometimes I might suppress the upper and lower limits of the summation if I think it won't sacrifice clarity: that is, instead of $\sum_{i=0}^m a_i$, I will just write $\sum a_i$.
